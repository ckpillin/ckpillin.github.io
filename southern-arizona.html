Rooster Cogburn Ostrich Ranch

While the ranch isn't a hike, our family has spent many hours exploring the Ostrich Ranch. We highly recommend it for families. There is a wide variety of animals to hand feed like donkeys, deer, goats, lorikeets, bunnies, ducks and of course, ostriches. Our daughter is a big fan of the Monster Truck ride out amongst the ostriches. They even sell ostrich eggs which are a particular favorite for us as scrambled eggs for a crowd or to make extra fluffy baked goods.

Chiricahua National Monument

The full loop at Chiricahua National Monument feels like wandering through another world. The moment you drop into the heart of the park, the landscape shifts into a maze of towering rock spires, balancing boulders, and formations so strange they almost look sculpted on purpose. It’s easy to see why people call this place a “Wonderland of Rocks”—every turn reveals something new and unexpected. The loop winds through canyons, ridges, and narrow passages where the rocks rise like stone sentinels on both sides. Some formations look like giant chess pieces, others like frozen waves, and some are so delicately balanced you can’t help but stop and stare. The mix of pine, oak, and open sky adds just enough greenery to make the whole scene feel even more surreal. What makes the hike special is how immersive it is. You’re not just viewing the formations from a distance—you’re walking right among them, weaving through clusters of hoodoos and squeezing between walls that have been shaped by centuries of wind and erosion. The trail has a rhythm to it: long stretches of quiet forest, sudden bursts of panoramic views, and then those tight, winding sections where the rocks feel close enough to touch. By the time you finish the loop, you feel like you’ve explored a hidden world tucked away in southeastern Arizona. It’s challenging in places, but the scenery is so unique and otherworldly that the miles seem to fly by. Chiricahua is one of those hikes that sticks with you long after you’ve left the rocks behind.

Wasson Peak - Saguaro National Park

Hiking Wasson Peak gives you the full Sonoran Desert experience from start to finish. The trail climbs steadily through a landscape dotted with towering saguaros, prickly pear, and desert shrubs that seem to glow in the sunlight. It’s a solid workout, but the scenery keeps you motivated the whole way up. Every time you look back, the view gets a little wider, stretching across the rolling hills and rugged ridgelines of Saguaro National Park. As you gain elevation, the saguaros thin out and the terrain shifts into rocky slopes with sweeping views in every direction. The higher you climb, the more Tucson begins to appear in the distance, framed by layers of mountains and desert valleys. It’s the kind of trail where you find yourself stopping often—not because you need a break, but because the landscape keeps giving you new reasons to pause. The final push to the summit is steep but rewarding. At the top, the panorama is huge: endless desert, distant peaks, and a full 360‑degree view that makes the whole climb feel worth it. There’s a quietness up there that settles in, even if other hikers are around. Wasson Peak is challenging without being overwhelming, and it showcases some of the best scenery the Tucson area has to offer. If you’re looking for a hike that blends effort with incredible desert views, this one delivers from the first step to the last.

Bull Pasture - Organ Pipe Cactus National Monument

Bull Pasture is a hike that sneaks up on you—in the best way. It starts out feeling pretty mellow, just a desert trail winding through organ pipe cacti and low shrubs, but before long you’re climbing into a landscape that feels wilder and more remote with every step. The higher you go, the more the desert stretches out behind you, a patchwork of greens, tans, and rugged ridgelines that seem to go on forever. What really stands out on this trail is the sense of space. The views open up early and just keep expanding, giving you that wide‑open Sonoran Desert feeling that’s hard to find anywhere else. The organ pipe cacti are everywhere, tall and branching, almost like they’re watching you make your way up the mountain. It’s a unique kind of beauty—quiet, raw, and full of character. As you approach Bull Pasture, the terrain shifts into cooler pockets of rock and shade, a welcome change after the steady climb. The overlook itself feels like a natural balcony hanging above the desert. It’s breezy, peaceful, and the kind of spot where you want to sit for a while before heading back down. The hike has some effort to it, but it’s the kind that feels rewarding rather than punishing. Between the sweeping views, the unusual cactus forests, and the sense of solitude, Bull Pasture offers a different side of Arizona—one that’s rugged, scenic, and absolutely worth the climb.

Picacho Peak

When hiking Picacho Peak the journey matters more than the pace, especially when you’re out with two four‑year‑olds who are just starting to explore the desert on their own terms. We took it slow—lots of stops to look at rocks, point out saguaros, and admire every lizard that darted across the trail. And honestly, that’s the perfect way to experience this place. The trail winds through classic Sonoran Desert scenery, with tall cacti standing like quiet guardians and wide views stretching toward the surrounding mountains. Even from the lower sections, the landscape feels big and open, full of warm colors and that soft desert breeze that makes everything feel calmer. The kids loved spotting the different shapes of the saguaros and calling out the “funny‑looking” ones, which kept the whole hike light and playful. As we made our way up, the peak loomed above us, but we never felt rushed. The trail has plenty to look at, and taking breaks just meant more time to soak in the scenery. The desert was bright and beautiful, and the kids were proud every time they climbed a rocky step or made it around a bend on their own. We didn’t push for the summit—this outing was about enjoying the desert together, not conquering anything. And in that sense, Picacho Peak delivered perfectly. It’s a great place for families to wander, explore, and appreciate the desert at a pace that lets everyone enjoy the day.

Kartchner Cavern

Kartchner Caverns isn’t a hike, but it’s absolutely worth the detour—especially if you’re curious about the kind of beauty that exists far below the desert floor. The guided tour leads you through winding passages of limestone formations that look almost soft in the low light, like they’ve been shaped by time rather than carved. It’s humid, warm, and strangely calming, a completely different world from the dry Arizona landscape above. One of the most memorable moments comes when the guide turns off the lights. It’s not just dark—it’s a total absence of light, the kind of darkness you can’t find anywhere on the surface. For a few seconds, you’re suspended in it. The air feels heavier, like it’s pressing gently against your skin. Your heartbeat suddenly seems louder, and there’s this mix of peace and unease that settles in at the same time. It’s a reminder of how rare true darkness is, and how alive your senses become when sight disappears. When the lights return, the formations seem even more dramatic—massive columns, delicate soda straws, and flowstone that looks like it’s still moving. Everything glistens slightly in the cave humidity, giving the whole place a quiet, otherworldly glow. Kartchner Caverns may not involve miles of trail, but it offers an experience that sticks with you. It’s a slow, thoughtful kind of adventure—one that invites you to step out of the sun, into the earth, and into a darkness that feels both ancient and strangely comforting.
